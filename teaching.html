Teaching Philosophy

Case-based and project-based. Students finish with a portfolio artifact that looks like what companies actually do — not just a grade.

The classroom reflects the real workplace

Most AI failures aren't technical — they're organizational. Bad use-case selection, poor success metrics, missing governance, or teams that don't trust the output. I build those scenarios into course discussions and assignments so students can practice navigating them before they're on the hook for real.

Judgment over tool worship

The specific tools will change. The judgment framework won't. Students learn how to evaluate when AI is the right choice, how to spot failure modes (hallucinations, bias, cost overruns), and how to design systems that stay safe as they scale. I don't teach students to be impressed by demos — I teach them to interrogate them.

Mixed backgrounds, same rigor

I teach to rooms with mixed technical backgrounds — and I've managed teams the same way for two decades. Non-technical students learn to frame and evaluate; technically stronger students learn to communicate and translate. Everyone leaves with something useful.

Courses I'd teach

Three concepts, ranging from business-school to professional certificate audience.

Course concept 1

Applied GenAI for Business & Operations

College of Business / IS — or CPaCE professional certificate

How to select, design, evaluate, and govern AI use cases inside real organizations. Covers the full lifecycle from "should we do this?" to "is it actually working?"

Learning outcomes

  • Evaluate AI use cases against business viability and technical feasibility
  • Design human-in-the-loop workflows with appropriate oversight
  • Define success metrics and build an evaluation plan
  • Identify and mitigate risk: privacy, bias, security, cost
  • Communicate AI proposals to non-technical stakeholders

Capstone deliverable

A use-case brief: problem statement, proposed solution, ROI estimate, evaluation plan, and governance checklist. Written for a non-technical executive audience.

Course concept 2

AI for Product, GTM, and Customer Success

College of Business / MBA elective

How AI changes go-to-market strategy, customer engagement, and product decisions. Covers adoption dynamics, value measurement, change management, and what separates pilots that scale from pilots that stall.

Learning outcomes

  • Map AI capabilities to GTM and customer success use cases
  • Build an adoption and change management plan
  • Design feedback loops and measurement frameworks
  • Navigate enterprise procurement and trust-building
  • Analyze case studies of AI adoption success and failure

Capstone deliverable

An AI adoption playbook for a specific GTM motion: target customer, value proposition, key risks, rollout plan, and success metrics. Based on a real company case.

Course concept 3

AI for Analysts & Power Users

CPaCE certificate — working professionals

Hands-on. Python notebooks, prompt engineering, retrieval concepts, automation, and quality control. Designed for analysts, ops leads, and knowledge workers who want to move beyond basic prompting.

Learning outcomes

  • Build and evaluate prompt workflows for real job tasks
  • Understand retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) concepts
  • Automate repetitive analytical work with Python
  • Apply quality control methods to AI-generated outputs
  • Manage cost, latency, and error tradeoffs in AI workflows

Capstone deliverable

A working prototype: a lightweight AI-assisted workflow relevant to the student's own job or domain — documented, tested, and presented with an honest assessment of limitations.
